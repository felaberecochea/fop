Honest Conversations with Scientists

Plants are everywhere. They feed us, sustain ecosystems, and shape the world we live in. Yet plant research tends to happen quietly behind laboratory doors, and we, the plant researchers, don’t often share it with the public. Why? Does it have to do with the type of research? With the public’s perception? Or maybe it is more complex? It certainly seems to be. Moreover, the main aim of plant research is to fulfil the goal of generating knowledge. Can plant research also have other purposes? In these eight posters, you can look at some of Utrecht University’s researchers’ and students’ answers to these questions. As you read through the exhibition, ponder if you share the same opinions. Enjoy the exhibition.

Click on any poster to view or download the full version.

Growing Tomatoes Without the Plant
Karl Strube
"This is our version of lab-grown meat, but for tomatoes."
Computational Modeling of Smart Plant Decisions
Kirsten ten Tusscher
"I want to show with my research that plants, which have no brains, can make smart decisions."
Cellular Programming and Patterning in Plants
Jason Gardiner
"Once we understand the programming, we will add our own programs to the code."
Strengthening Roots Against Drought and Parasites
Dorota Kawa
"My work on witchweed-resistant sorghum can improve the lives of many farmers."
The Evolution of Protective Root Barriers
Kaisa Kajala
"In principle, my research is simple to explain: plants with stronger roots are better able to withstand drought."
Plant Resilience Against Infections and Disease
Dmitry Lapin
"By presenting everything too simplistically, we risk losing those who are genuinely interested in a deeper understanding of botany."
Plant Responses to Environmental Water Stress
Rashmi Sasidharan
"As scientists we have a great responsibility to speak out about how important it is to invest in new technologies."
Cellular Communication in Roots During Flooding
Gyöngyi Macías Honti
"I want to know what happens at the cellular level in the root during flooding... and how they communicate with each other under these conditions."
